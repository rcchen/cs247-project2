Needfinding: Users and the Context of Use

In the process of discovering our unique public experience, we observed that the experience of waiting in line offered an opportunity for transforming static, passive time, into something collaborative, creative, and much more interesting.

Waiting in line is very rarely enjoyable and even less so because of the perception that one is wasting otherwise valuable time while standing in a line. Consider that standing is not conducive to working on a computer, for instance, or even reading a book long enough to make it through more than a few chapters. Moreover, a sense of unfamiliarity often accompanies this feeling of helpless unproductivity due to an immersion among strangers that one tends to be standing next to while waiting in a line.

Research confirmed by MIT operations researcher Richard Larson suggests that much of the dissatisfaction with waiting in lines has to do with how occupied time feels shorter than unoccupied time even if the occupied time is spent doing something as simple as looking at oneself in a mirror. In other words, if we provide something with which to occupy persons waiting in a line, their experience will be more positive, ultimately leading to happier people and fresher environments.

Our observations and conversations coupled with personal experiences provided an insight into an unmet need. A representative comment about standing in lines we noted was, "In lines, I always get upset thinking about all the other things I could be doing". Would it not be much better to engage in something creative, simply fun, and collaborative rather than passively wait whilst surrounded by strangers who you are not sure are interested in talking to you?

Sketching

Before touching a single line of code, our group got together and brainstormed a bunch of ideas. At the core, our goal was to design and build an activity that was interesting and entertaining. When we delve into the essence of our mission, the need we are targeting is really people's seemingly inherent thirst to be entertained and violent aversion to undesired inactivity (i.e. boredom). Here are some of the ideas that we had during our brainstorming portion of the assignment. We have also included some of our insights as well as how they (do not quite) address user needs and behavior.

Final decision

Rationale:

As we considered our context, audience, and goals carefully, we realized we were looking for several criteria. We wanted an experience that didn't require a whole lot of moving around (as people stand in place in line), could be used by one or more people at the same time, but did not require more than one person, was interactive and engaging, and had no clear beginning or end (more like a piece of art). Additionally, we took into account the secondary factors of technical limitations and our own time constraints. We finally chose the idea of having balls fall from the sky, and play different notes when they bounce off of a person's shadow based on where they are on the screen. The balls change color every time they play a different note from the one they last played. This fulfilled our requirements for a good in-line experience. It can be used by one or mre people, and does not require people to move much at all. Additionally, it is engaging on two different levels: initially, it is novel and interesting solely because of the changing colors and the interactivity of balls bouncing off of one's shadow. However, if someone spends a long time interacting with it, they might start to notice that the tone played by each ball is linked to its location when it strikes the shadow, and manipulate their shadow to produce certain tones.

We settled on the idea of allowing shadows to allow make music for individuals waiting in line. Using a concept similar to sound drop.

The application would allow the user to interact with balls coming down using their shadow as a means to make fun sounds.

The application we created had the following key characteristics:

  • The balls would bounce off the shadow based on the angle at which they hit the shadow
  • Each ball would make a sound determined by the position on the screen at which they hit the shadow
  • The balls would behave realistically so as to account for gravity and drag whilst moving through the screen

Key Design Considerations and implementation:

We wanted the app to be as intuitive as possible and not require unexpected extraneous interactions which would distract from the key purpose of the app. As a result we tried to make the interaction of the user's shadow with the ball as natural as possible. To enable this to happen we made the following design decisions:

  • Include many pixels as contact indicators on the ball, to cause impact to be as latent free as possible even though the responsiveness to the shadow is slightly delayed. The key purpose of this was to ensure that the ball would respond to the shadow as a surface even though the user made sudden unexpected collisions with the ball
  • Users expect balls to have gravity, and return back after a bounce in addition to expecting the ball to bounce in the correct direction based on impact with the shadow. To make sure this happened we included a few parameters that would constantly check for the nature of collision as well as keep adjusting the gravity, drag and other parameters to make the balls move as users expect them to. The purpose of this was to allow users to predict where the ball would move and include a fun, realistic model in the app.
  • Edit sound files to be of a similar scale-range to not include disharmonious sounds and effects when the balls hit different parts of the shadow. To be able to do this, we edited sound files and made sure the range was not too extreme and calibrated the different parts of a shadow based on location on the screen to be able to make fun sounds that would not be annoying to hear.
  • Finally, aesthetic value is key in such an app and so along with smooth sounds, realistic physical engine and good response to shadow we added colors to balls that would change based on note to increase the visual appeal of the app and give the user the sense of the dominant note at a point in time.
